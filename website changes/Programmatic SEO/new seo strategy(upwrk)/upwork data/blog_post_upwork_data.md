# We Analyzed 45,742 AI Jobs on Upwork. The Data Shows a Market Splitting in Two.

**By Agentic AI Labs | March 2026**
**Based on: 45,742 unique job postings, July 2025 – February 2026**

---

We didn't set out to build a market intelligence report. We set up a Telegram bot to track AI job postings on Upwork across three accounts — because we wanted to know where the work was coming from.

Eight months later, we had 45,742 unique job postings.

We ran the analysis. What came back wasn't a simple picture of a growing market. It was a picture of a market at an inflection point — and the data tells a story that most AI agencies aren't paying attention to yet.

Here's what we found.

---

## The Market Peaked in September 2025 — and Nobody Talked About It

The first thing that stood out was the volume curve.

| Month | AI / Automation Jobs | Change |
|---|---|---|
| July 2025 | ~3,200 | baseline |
| August 2025 | ~4,600 | +44% |
| **September 2025** | **~5,100** | **peak** |
| October 2025 | ~4,700 | -8% |
| November 2025 | ~3,900 | -17% |
| December 2025 | ~3,200 | -18% |
| January 2026 | ~1,500 | -53% |
| February 2026 | ~800 | -47% |

AI and automation job postings peaked in September 2025 and fell roughly 75% by February 2026.

The easy interpretation is "the market is cooling." The more accurate interpretation, when you look at what the data says about *pricing* alongside volume, is more uncomfortable: the bottom of the market is being washed out. The commodity layer is contracting. Something is being sorted.

---

## The Median Job Pays $300

Here is the number that matters most, and it barely gets discussed.

Of the 42,750 fixed-price jobs in our dataset with pricing data:

- **Median fixed price: $300**
- Average fixed price: $1,362 (pulled up by outliers)
- 89% of hourly jobs: under $30/hr

The $300 median is not a starting point for negotiation. It is the price that the largest single group of Upwork buyers has decided "AI automation" is worth. And it has been compressing.

For context: web design agencies in 1998 charged $25,000–$50,000 for a website. By 2005, the same work cost $3,000–$8,000. Today, Squarespace charges $12 a month.

The AI automation market is following the same curve. The price compression isn't a market anomaly — it's a market signal.

The question is what it signals.

---

## n8n Had 2,919 Job Postings. GoHighLevel Had 2,185.

The most-demanded specific skills in the dataset (excluding generic terms like "automation" and "developer") reveal exactly what buyers think they want:

1. **n8n** — 2,919 jobs
2. **GoHighLevel / GHL** — 2,185 jobs (combined)
3. **Integration** (general) — 2,952 jobs
4. **Voice** — 1,050 jobs
5. **CRM** — 1,159 jobs
6. **Zapier** — 697 jobs

The n8n number is the most telling. In July 2025, n8n appeared in 349 job titles. By February 2026, that had dropped to 74.

n8n isn't getting less popular as a tool — it's getting more capable and easier to use. Which means the thing clients were paying agencies to do is becoming something they can do themselves, or something cheaper overseas teams can do, or something a better tool can handle with a few clicks.

The moat of "I know how to build [n8n workflows](https://www.tryagentikai.com/ai-workflow-automation-n8n)" has a short shelf life. The data says it's already shortening.

## The Most Interesting Number in the Entire Dataset

Here is the data point that stopped us:

- **"chatbot"** — 946 jobs
- **"ai agent"** — 945 jobs

Virtually identical. One job apart.

In every technology market, there's a moment when the old vocabulary and the new vocabulary have equal search demand. That moment is the inflection point of a vocabulary shift. It's when the old concept hasn't died yet, but the new one has fully arrived.

The dot-com equivalent happened around 2003-2004, when "website" and "web application" achieved parity in job listings. It signaled that the market had moved past novelty and into sophistication. Clients who understood the difference between a website and a web application had the bigger budgets — and they were looking for agencies who understood it too.

"Chatbot" is the old vocabulary. "AI agent" is the new one. They're tied right now.

The buyers who search "[AI agent](https://www.tryagentikai.com/glossary/ai-agent-vs-ai-system)" instead of "chatbot" understand what they're asking for. They've already tried the $300 chatbot. They're asking for something different — a system that can reason, act, and integrate. They have the budget to match.

And there were 945 of them posting jobs in an 8-month window on a single platform.

## Voice AI: 1,050 Jobs, Near-Zero Supply

The keyword "voice" appeared in 1,050 job postings across the dataset. That number held more consistently than almost any other category — 144 jobs in July 2025, still 49 in February 2026 when overall volume had collapsed.

Voice AI demand is sticky. It didn't follow the same decline curve as generic automation.

The reason is simple: voice AI is genuinely hard. The technical stack — [Vapi](https://www.tryagentikai.com/vapi-alternative), ElevenLabs, Twilio, LangChain, CRM webhooks — is not trivial to assemble. Latency is a real engineering problem. Conversation design is a discipline that most developers don't have. The gap between a voice bot that technically works and a voice agent that a real customer will tolerate on a business call is enormous.

We ran searches. The number of Upwork profiles specifically positioned for end-to-end voice AI systems — not just "I can build a voice bot" but packaged, productized voice AI — is near zero.

1,050 jobs worth of demand. Near-zero specialized supply. That gap doesn't stay open forever.

---

## 52% of Buyers Want "Expert" Level. Zero Want Beginners.

From our dataset's experience level breakdown:

- Expert: 23,300 jobs (52%)
- Intermediate: 21,293 jobs (48%)
- Entry Level: 2 jobs (0%)

The floor of acceptable has risen. Dramatically.

Two years ago, knowing "how to set up automation" was enough. Now clients posting on Upwork have already tried the basic version — they did it themselves with a tutorial, or hired someone cheap who didn't deliver. They're not looking for someone to explain what automation is. They're looking for someone who can solve a specific problem they couldn't solve the first time.

The entry-level market for AI agency work has effectively disappeared. The shakeout of basic "automation freelancers" has already happened — or is in the process of happening right now.

## The Two Markets That Are Emerging

After eight months of data, the picture that emerges is not a single AI services market. It's two markets that are diverging.

**Market 1 — The commodity layer**

Generic automation work. n8n workflows, chatbot setups, GoHighLevel configurations. Buyers who found this on Upwork, searched for the cheapest option, and posted with a $300 budget. This market is compressing in price, increasing in competition from offshore teams, and will increasingly be eaten by better no-code tools.

The data: median $300, 89% of hourly work under $30/hr, volume declining 75% from peak.

**Market 2 — The systems layer**

Buyers who understand the difference between a chatbot and an AI agent. Who are searching for "voice AI for appointment setting" or "AI system for real estate lead qualification" or "[replace SDR team with AI](https://www.tryagentikai.com/ai-sdr-for-ghl-agencies)." Who have failed with the cheap version and are looking for someone who can build something that actually integrates with their operations and delivers a measurable result.

The data: 945 "ai agent" jobs with presumably higher budgets, 1,050 voice jobs with near-zero specialized supply, 32,635 jobs from clients who have already spent over $1,000 on Upwork (71% of the entire dataset).

These two markets require completely different positioning, different pricing, and different work. The agencies that try to serve both will end up competing on price in Market 1 while failing to win in Market 2.

## What the Dot-Com Parallel Actually Predicts

In 2001, the web design industry went through the same compression. Agencies that built generic websites for anyone with a budget watched their $25,000 projects become $3,000 projects become $500 templates.

The ones that survived didn't fight the compression. They moved above it.

They stopped being "web agencies" and became "healthcare web agencies" or "e-commerce conversion agencies." They stopped charging for building a website and started charging for what the website produced — leads, conversions, revenue. They stopped doing one-off projects and built retainer relationships where they maintained, improved, and measured the systems they had built.

The tool became invisible. The outcome became the product.

The companies that did this by 2002 survived. The ones still building generic websites in 2003 competed on price until they couldn't.

The AI agency market is in 2001 right now. The data says so.

## The Specific Gaps the Data Reveals (and Nobody Is Filling)

Across 45,742 job postings, five gaps appear consistently:

**1. Outcome-based positioning** — Every profile says "I build AI automations with n8n." Zero profiles say "I reduce your client acquisition cost by 40% using AI-powered outreach — guaranteed." The tool-first vs. outcome-first divide is almost total. The buyers with real budgets want the outcome framing.

**2. Vertical-specific AI systems** — The winning agencies post-dot-com were the ones who picked one industry and went impossibly deep. The same play is wide open right now. An AI system built specifically for real estate lead qualification will outperform a generic automation for that use case every time — and the specialized agency can charge accordingly.

**3. [Voice AI as a productized service](https://www.tryagentikai.com/ai-voice-agent)** — 1,050 jobs, near-zero specialized providers. This is the clearest supply-demand gap in the entire dataset.

**4. Human-in-the-loop architecture** — The hardest engineering problem in production AI: designing the handoff layer where AI does 80% autonomously and routes the critical 20% to a human with full context. Nobody is positioning specifically for this. Every serious AI deployment at scale needs it.

**5. AI governance and monitoring** — Only 33% of developers fully trust AI outputs. AI-generated code has 1.7x more defects without proper review. Non-technical business owners deploying AI agents have no visibility into whether their systems are working or failing silently. The "AI system maintenance and monitoring" retainer doesn't exist yet as a standard offering. It will.

## What This Means If You're Building in This Space

The $300 median is not an insult. It's market information.

It tells you exactly where not to compete, and exactly where to go instead.

The buyers searching for $300 chatbots and generic n8n workflows are real buyers — but they are the buyers whose problems have already been commoditized. The tools will solve those problems more cheaply than any agency can.

The buyers searching for AI systems that integrate with their operations, replace headcount, generate measurable outcomes, and need to keep working reliably for the next two years — those buyers exist too. The data shows 1,000+ of them per month on a single platform.

They just can't find what they're looking for.
